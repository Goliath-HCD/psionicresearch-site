The McMinnville Photographs: America's Most Credible UFO Evidence

Updated March 2026 · 7 min read · Photographic & Scientific Record

Of the thousands of UFO photographs taken during the twentieth century, the vast majority have been debunked, dismissed, or simply forgotten. The McMinnville photographs are the exception. Taken on the evening of May 11, 1950, by Oregon farmer Paul Trent and his wife Evelyn, these two black-and-white images have survived more than seventy years of scientific scrutiny — and remain, in the assessment of multiple independent analyses, genuinely unexplained.

That judgment was not made by credulous enthusiasts. It was made by photogrammetric analysts working under contract to the United States Air Force and the University of Colorado, by image processing scientists at NASA's Jet Propulsion Laboratory, and by French aerospace analysts using twenty-first-century digital reconstruction tools. The McMinnville photographs are not a fringe curiosity. They are, in the words of one Condon Committee scientist, "the most genuine-appearing UFO photographs in the files."

The Evening of May 11, 1950

Paul and Evelyn Trent were farmers in the Willamette Valley outside McMinnville, Oregon. On the evening of May 11, Evelyn was feeding rabbits near the farmhouse when she saw a large, disc-shaped object moving silently through the sky to the northwest. She called to Paul, who retrieved the family's Roamer camera and took two photographs before the object accelerated out of sight.

The photographs show a clearly disc-shaped object with a superstructure on top — a pronounced circular form with what appears to be a raised center section or cupola. The object appears to be metallic, with a consistent surface texture visible under magnification. Both photographs were taken within seconds of each other, and the apparent position of the object relative to background elements — the farmhouse eaves, the telephone wires, the horizon — is geometrically consistent between frames.

Paul Trent finished the roll of film, had it developed, and initially showed the images only to neighbors and family. The local newspaper, the McMinnville Telephone-Register, published them in June 1950. Within days, the Portland Oregonian picked up the story. A week later, Life magazine ran the photographs in its June 26, 1950 issue — reaching millions of readers and establishing the images as the most widely seen UFO photographs in American history.

The Condon Committee Analysis

In 1966, the U.S. Air Force commissioned the University of Colorado to conduct an independent scientific investigation of UFO reports — what became known as the Condon Committee, led by physicist Edward U. Condon. The committee's final report, published in 1969, was broadly skeptical of UFOs as a phenomenon — but it contained a crucial anomaly.

Photogrammetric analyst William Hartmann, working for the committee, subjected the McMinnville photographs to careful geometric and optical analysis. His conclusion, published in the Condon Report, was unambiguous:

"This is one of the few UFO reports in which all factors investigated, geometric, psychological, and physical, appear to be consistent with the assertion that an extraordinary flying object, silvered and disc-shaped, was photographed."

— William Hartmann, Condon Committee, 1969

Hartmann's analysis examined the angular size of the object, its apparent distance from the camera based on perspective geometry, and the consistency of the lighting between the two frames. He found no evidence of a hoax and no conventional explanation for what the photographs depicted.

The Sheaffer Hypothesis and Its Limits

The most prominent skeptical analysis was advanced by Robert Sheaffer in the 1970s, who argued that the photographs were taken in the morning rather than the evening — based on the direction of shadows beneath the telephone wires — and that the object was most likely a truck mirror or other suspended object photographed near the farmhouse. Sheaffer's shadow analysis was subsequently disputed by multiple researchers who examined the original negatives and determined that the shadow direction was consistent with overcast late-afternoon conditions as reported.

The "suspended model" hypothesis faces a specific geometric problem: the underside of the object shows no string, wire, or support mechanism under magnification, and the surface texture visible in the original negatives is inconsistent with a hand-fabricated model of the era. The object's apparent size, calculated from the geometry of the eaves and wires in both frames, places it at a distance of several hundred feet — far too distant for a hand-thrown or suspended object of the dimensions visible in the photographs.

The IPACO Study: Twenty-First Century Analysis

In 2013, the French organization IPACO (Image Processing and Analysis Company) published a comprehensive digital reconstruction of the McMinnville photographs using modern photogrammetric software and high-resolution scans of the original negatives. The IPACO team applied the same analytical methodologies used to authenticate forensic and satellite imagery.

Their findings were mixed but significant. The IPACO team concluded that the object in the photographs was not consistent with a distant large craft — their analysis suggested it was more likely a small object close to the camera. But crucially, their analysis could not identify what that small object was, found no evidence of wire or support, and was unable to match the object's surface characteristics to any known manufactured item from the period.

The McMinnville photographs remain, in the formal scientific record, unexplained — not "proven genuine" as UAP advocates sometimes claim, but genuinely and rigorously unresolved after more than seven decades of analysis by credentialed scientists using the best available tools.

Why McMinnville Still Matters

The significance of the McMinnville photographs lies not in what they prove — which remains uncertain — but in what they demonstrate about how UFO evidence is evaluated. The case attracted serious scientific attention, produced conflicting but not dismissive analysis from credentialed researchers, and has survived repeated attempts at definitive debunking. It stands as the clearest example in the photographic record of what genuine scientific uncertainty about an anomalous aerial observation actually looks like — a thread that runs from 1950 through the 2004 USS Nimitz encounter and into the PURSUE files released in 2026.

McMinnville, Oregon, now hosts an annual UFO festival in honor of the Trent photographs — drawing researchers, enthusiasts, and curious onlookers to a town whose most enduring claim to fame remains two grainy black-and-white photographs taken by a farmer who had no particular reason to lie.
